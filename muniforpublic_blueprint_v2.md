# MuniForPublic — Project Blueprint

---

## 1. What This Is

MuniForPublic is a civic intelligence platform for the question: **What would it take to make San Francisco's Muni transit system free?**

It is not a document archive. It is not a research paper. It is a tool that lets future policymakers, organizers, and staffers ask real questions and get structured, sourced, strategically useful answers.

The platform has two layers:

- **5 interactive blocks** — each one a clickable module that opens into a structured knowledge base covering a different dimension of the problem
- **A central chatbot** — pulls from all 5 blocks to answer natural language policy questions, tailored to who's asking

The thesis: public-interest policy ideas fail not because they're wrong, but because the knowledge needed to execute them is scattered across documents, people, institutions, and timing. This platform makes that knowledge legible.

---

## 2. Why This Matters Right Now

SFMTA faces a projected $307 million structural deficit beginning July 2026, growing to $434 million by July 2030. The agency is actively searching for new revenue measures. MTC recently structured a Bay Area transit loan (not a grant) with 12-year repayment terms. Fare policy is already a live political conversation.

Meanwhile, free Muni for youth (18 and under) already exists as a functioning program — meaning the question isn't hypothetical. There's a real policy precedent, real implementation experience, and real campaign memory from the people who made it happen.

The opportunity is not just "prove free Muni is good." It's to build the infrastructure that lets future decision-makers answer: **Who matters, what evidence exists, what objections recur, what funding routes are real, and what political window would make this viable?**

---

## 3. Who This Is For

Three distinct users. Every design decision should serve at least one of them.

### The Organizer
Community advocates, coalition leaders, transit justice groups.
**Needs:** coalition maps, effective framing, objection handling scripts, precedent stories, campaign sequencing advice.

### The Policy Staffer
Legislative aides, budget analysts, agency staff, policy researchers.
**Needs:** funding eligibility details, legal constraints, implementation pathways, comparable city data, fiscal impact estimates.

### The Elected Official (or Candidate)
Supervisors, mayoral candidates, board members.
**Needs:** concise narrative, political upside/risk assessment, fiscal credibility, talking points, likely opposition preview.

## 4. The 5 Blocks

The landing screen shows 5 blocks. Each is a door into one dimension of the problem. Users can browse each block independently, or let the chatbot pull from them in combination.

---

### Block 1: HISTORY & PRECEDENT

**The question it answers:** What's been tried before — here and elsewhere — and what actually happened?

**What's inside:**

**Campaign timeline**
A chronological reconstruction of every relevant moment in SF's free transit history: the youth free Muni campaign, senior discount programs, COVID fare suspension, any past proposals that failed or stalled. Each entry captures not just what happened, but why — what triggered it, who drove it, what enabled or killed it.

**City precedents**
Structured profiles of other cities that have implemented fare-free transit, partial or full. Kansas City (full system, 2020), Richmond (youth, pilot), Tallinn (residents, 2013), Luxembourg (national, 2020), and others. Each tagged by: scale, population comparability to SF, funding mechanism, political context, outcomes, and what's transferable vs. what isn't.

**Campaign memory**
Informal strategic knowledge captured from interviews with organizers and insiders:
- Which arguments actually moved decision-makers
- Which arguments sounded right but failed in practice
- Who unexpectedly blocked progress
- Which staffers were technically helpful behind the scenes
- What sequencing worked (and what ordering mistakes were made)
- What coalition gaps killed momentum
- What compromises made a pilot possible
- What language alienated moderates
- What external events created unexpected leverage

This is distinct from formal research. It's movement memory — and it disappears when organizers move on.

---

### Block 2: STAKEHOLDER & POWER MAP

**The question it answers:** Who decides, who funds, who blocks, who champions — and in what order?

**What's inside:**

**Entity and individual profiles**
Every organization and key individual in the decision chain, each tagged across multiple dimensions:

| Dimension | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Formal authority | Can they legally approve this? | SFMTA Board can approve fare changes |
| Budget authority | Do they control relevant funding? | Board of Supervisors approves city budget |
| Agenda-setting power | Can they put this on the table? | A supervisor can introduce a resolution |
| Veto risk | Can they kill it? | Mayor can veto legislation |
| Coalition value | Does winning them unlock other allies? | Labor endorsement signals broad support |
| Narrative influence | Do they shape public perception? | Major newspaper editorial board |
| Timing sensitivity | Are they under pressure right now? | Supervisor in contested re-election |

**Relationship map**
Who influences whom. Not just org charts, but real influence flows: which community groups have the ear of which supervisors, which agencies defer to which funding bodies, which staffers are gatekeepers.

**Decision sequence**
The specific institutional path a free Muni proposal would need to travel:
1. SFMTA staff develops proposal
2. SFMTA Board reviews and approves fare policy change
3. Funding source identified and secured (varies — could require Board of Supervisors vote, ballot measure, regional approval)
4. City budget process incorporates ongoing cost
5. Implementation by SFMTA operations

**Key entities to map:**
SFMTA (staff and board), Mayor's Office, Board of Supervisors (individual members), SFCTA, MTC, community coalitions (transit justice, youth, disability, labor, senior, environmental), business groups

---

### Block 3: FUNDING PATHWAYS

**The question it answers:** Where could the money come from — and what's actually viable?

**What's inside:**

**Funding source inventory**
Every plausible revenue source, each profiled with:
- Legal eligibility — can it fund fare-free transit operations?
- Operating vs. capital — does it cover ongoing costs?
- Recurring vs. one-time — sustainable or just a pilot?
- Vote threshold — simple majority? supermajority? ballot?
- Political feasibility — who supports, who opposes?
- Precedent — has this been used for similar programs?

**Funding source categories:**
- Regional transportation sales taxes
- Local parcel tax or general fund revenue
- Climate, emissions, and congestion pricing funds
- Health and public health funding streams
- School access and youth mobility framing
- Employer-based mechanisms
- Philanthropy and foundation grants
- State pilot programs and innovation funds
- Institutional transit pass partnerships

**Funding stack scenarios**
Plausible combinations of sources — no single source covers the full cost, so this module shows which stacks could work together.

**Fiscal context**
Current SFMTA financial picture: the projected deficit, farebox revenue figures, what fare-free would cost annually. Grounds every conversation in real numbers.

---

### Block 4: OBJECTION & REBUTTAL BANK

**The question it answers:** What will people say against this — and what's the best response for the room you're in?

**What's inside:**

**Structured objection cards**
Every recurring argument against fare-free Muni:

| Field | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Objection | The claim itself, stated as opponents would say it |
| Who makes it | Fiscal conservatives? Transit operators? Business groups? |
| Value claimed | What principle it appeals to |
| Evidence-based rebuttal | Strongest factual response, with sources |
| Rebuttal weakness | Where the rebuttal is incomplete (honesty builds credibility) |
| Reframe by audience | Same rebuttal, rewritten for different listeners |
| City precedent | What happened elsewhere |

**The core objection classes:**
1. "Muni can't afford it"
2. "Fund service quality first"
3. "Fare-free creates overcrowding"
4. "Free benefits people who don't need it"
5. "Better to target discounts"
6. "Fare evasion is already a problem"
7. "Operations, not fare policy, is the real issue"

**Audience-specific response packets**
The critical feature: the same objection gets a different best response depending on the audience. "Muni can't afford it" needs cost data for a fiscal moderate, worker impact framing for a labor ally, the youth free Muni precedent for a youth coalition, and political upside for a mayoral staffer.

---

### Block 5: POLITICAL TIMING & TRIGGERS

**The question it answers:** When does this become viable — and what should we be watching for?

**What's inside:**

**Condition tracker**
External conditions that affect viability:
- Fiscal environment (SFMTA deficit severity, new revenue proposals)
- Electoral calendar (mayoral race, supervisor elections, ballot deadlines)
- Public sentiment triggers (service failures, enforcement controversies, ridership milestones)
- Institutional shifts (new agency leadership, new committee chairs)
- Policy windows (budget cycle openings, regional plan updates, climate mandates)
- External shocks (federal funding changes, state policy shifts)

**Trigger events**
Specific events that create or close strategic openings, each tagged with: how it affects viability, which stakeholders it activates, what action it enables, and how long the window stays open.

**Upcoming calendar**
Known dates that matter: budget deadlines, elections, ballot filing dates, board meeting schedules.

## 5. The Chatbot

Sits at the center. Pulls from all 5 blocks.

### How it works

**Step 1: Query classification**
User types a question. The system determines which blocks are relevant.

| Question type | Primary blocks | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Funding | Funding + Stakeholders | "What sources could fund a senior pilot?" |
| Stakeholder | Stakeholders + Timing | "Who would need to approve a fare change?" |
| Objection | Objections + Funding + Precedent | "How do I respond to 'Muni can't afford it'?" |
| Timing | Timing + Stakeholders | "Is the current environment favorable?" |
| Precedent | History + Funding | "How did youth free Muni get passed?" |
| Strategy | All 5 blocks | "What would it take to make Muni free for seniors?" |

**Step 2: Retrieval**
Queries the database using semantic search (pgvector similarity) combined with structured filters (e.g., only "operating" + "recurring" funding sources for a sustainability question).

**Step 3: Response generation**
Retrieved data + user question + audience mode → Claude API → structured, sourced answer.

**Step 4: Citation and linking**
Every claim links back to its source. Clicking a source opens the relevant block view.

### Audience modes

A toggle that changes tone, emphasis, and format:

**Organizer mode** — leads with framing and narrative, emphasizes coalition implications, includes objection scripts, cites campaign memory

**Policy staffer mode** — leads with data and constraints, emphasizes legal eligibility and fiscal mechanics, includes comparable cities with caveats

**Elected official mode** — leads with narrative and political calculus, keeps it concise, includes talking points and likely opposition

## 6. Tech Stack (Entirely Free)

Every tool below is free-tier. No credit card required for MVP.

| Layer | Tool | Free tier |
|---|---|---|
| Database + backend | Supabase | 500MB database, pgvector included, Edge Functions, unlimited API requests |
| Embeddings | Google Gemini API | Free tier: 15 requests/min, generous daily token limit — use for generating embeddings and as the LLM |
| AI / chatbot LLM | Google Gemini API (gemini-2.0-flash) | Same free tier — handles query classification + response generation |
| Frontend + hosting | Vercel | Free for personal projects, auto-deploys from GitHub |
| Repo | GitHub | Free |
| Data entry | Google Sheets | Free — Person 2 works here, never touches code |

**Why this stack works at zero cost:**
- Supabase free tier includes Postgres with pgvector — so semantic search works without paying for a vector database
- Supabase Edge Functions replace the need for a separate Fastify server — they run serverless on Supabase's infra for free
- Google Gemini API free tier is generous enough for a prototype (the chatbot won't be handling thousands of queries)
- Vercel free tier hosts React apps with zero config
- No separate embedding service needed — Gemini API generates embeddings too

**Architecture simplified:**
```
React app (Vercel)
    ↓
Supabase Edge Functions (API logic, chatbot pipeline)
    ↓
Supabase Postgres + pgvector (all data, semantic search)
    ↓
Google Gemini API (embeddings, query classification, response generation)
```

No servers to manage. No bills.

## 8. Roles and Responsibilities

This project has three roles. The work that makes this platform actually valuable — the knowledge inside it and the intelligence layer on top — belongs to Persons 2 and 3.

---

### Person 1: Scaffolding

Sets up the infrastructure that Persons 2 and 3 work on top of.

**What they do:**
- Create the GitHub repo and project structure
- Set up the Supabase project, create all database tables from the schema above
- Build the React frontend: 5-block landing page, internal card/list/map views per block
- Create Google Sheet templates (one tab per table, column headers matching the schema exactly) for Person 2
- Write a simple import script that takes Person 2's Google Sheet exports and populates Supabase
- Deploy to Vercel
- Support Persons 2 and 3 when they hit infrastructure issues

**What they don't own:**
The quality of the data or the intelligence of the chatbot. Those are the hard parts.

---

### Person 2: Knowledge & Research (the foundation of the whole system)

This is the role that determines whether the platform is actually useful or just a nice-looking empty shell. Every answer the chatbot gives is only as good as what Person 2 puts into the database.

**What they do:**

**Process existing sources into structured data**
- Take the Sheila Evans interview and break it into: discrete claims (with confidence levels), stakeholder entries (with authority tags), campaign memory entries (with strategic lessons), timeline events
- Take SFMTA budget documents and extract: fiscal context numbers, funding source profiles, timeline events
- Take the youth free Muni history and structure it as: precedent cards, campaign memory, claims about what worked

**Research and write original structured entries**
- Profile 15–20 stakeholders with real assessments of their authority, veto risk, coalition value — this requires political judgment, not just Googling
- Write the 7 core objection cards with honest rebuttals AND honest weakness assessments — and then write 3 audience-specific response variants for each (21 response packets total)
- Research 5–8 city precedents and assess their real comparability to SF — not just "this city did it" but "here's what transfers and what doesn't"
- Map the decision pathway: the actual sequence of institutional steps a fare policy change would require

**Quality control**
- Every claim needs a source link
- Every stakeholder assessment needs justification
- Every rebuttal needs to acknowledge where it's weak
- Confidence levels must be honest (don't mark something "high" just because we want it to be true)

**What makes this role hard:**
This is editorial, analytical work. It requires reading dense budget documents, understanding political relationships, making judgment calls about feasibility, and writing clearly under structured constraints. It's research + political analysis + structured writing, not just data entry.

**Data targets:**

| Data type | Target | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Claims | 30–40 | High — the chatbot retrieves these |
| Sources | 15–20 | High — everything needs attribution |
| Stakeholders | 15–20 | High — the power map depends on this |
| Stakeholder relationships | 20–30 | Medium — enriches the map |
| Funding sources | 7–10 | High — core to the funding block |
| Objections | 7 core | High — the rebuttal bank |
| Audience response packets | 21 (7 × 3) | High — this is what makes the chatbot audience-aware |
| Precedents | 5–8 | Medium — supports arguments |
| Timeline events | 10–15 | Medium — historical + upcoming |
| Campaign memory | 5–10 | Medium — unique institutional knowledge |
| Decision pathway steps | 5–7 | Medium — maps the approval sequence |

---

### Person 3: Chatbot & Intelligence Layer (what makes this more than a database)

Without this role, the platform is just a browsable set of tables. Person 3 turns it into something that thinks — that takes a real question and assembles a strategic answer from structured knowledge.

**What they do:**

**Build query classification**
When a user types a question, determine what kind of question it is and which database tables to query. This is a prompt engineering task — write a system prompt for Gemini that classifies questions into types (funding, stakeholder, objection, timing, precedent, cross-cutting strategy) and returns which blocks to retrieve from.

**Build the retrieval pipeline**
For each question type, define the retrieval strategy:
- Semantic search: generate an embedding for the user's question, find the most similar claims/sources/objections via pgvector
- Structured filters: if it's a funding question, filter to operating + recurring sources; if it's a stakeholder question, filter by authority type
- Cross-block joins: if the question is "who controls the funding that could pay for this," you need to join funding sources → stakeholders

This is the architectural core. The retrieval pipeline determines whether the chatbot gives a relevant answer or a generic one.

**Build response generation**
Write the system prompts for Gemini that take retrieved context + user question + audience mode and generate a structured answer. This is three different prompt architectures:
- Organizer mode: narrative-first, coalition-aware, includes scripts and framing advice
- Staffer mode: data-first, technically precise, includes constraints and caveats
- Official mode: concise, politically aware, includes talking points

**Build the chat interface**
A React component: text input, audience mode toggle (3 options), response display with source citations that link back to block views.

**Handle edge cases**
- Questions where no relevant data exists → honest "we don't have enough information on this yet" with a note on what data is missing
- Ambiguous questions → classify best-effort, note the interpretation
- Cross-cutting strategy questions → pull from all 5 blocks, synthesize

**Iterate on quality**
After the first end-to-end test, the main work is prompt tuning: are the responses relevant? Are citations accurate? Does audience mode actually change the output meaningfully? Are there hallucinations?

**What makes this role hard:**
Prompt engineering for retrieval-augmented generation is finicky. The difference between a chatbot that gives generic AI answers and one that gives genuinely useful policy-specific answers is in the retrieval precision and the prompt architecture. This requires patience, testing, and a lot of iteration.

## 9. Two-Week Sprint

### Days 1–3: Foundation

**Person 1:**
- Repo, Supabase project, all tables created
- React shell with 5-block landing page
- Google Sheet templates delivered to Person 2
- Import script working

**Person 2:**
- Receives Google Sheet templates on day 1
- Starts immediately with Sheila interview → claims, stakeholders, campaign memory
- Moves to SFMTA budget documents → fiscal context, funding sources
- First batch ready for import by end of day 3

**Person 3:**
- Reads this blueprint thoroughly
- Studies the schema
- Designs prompt architecture on paper: classification categories, retrieval strategy per question type, response templates per audience mode
- Sets up Gemini API access (free tier)

**End of day 3:** Repo is live. First data batch is imported. Person 3 has their prompt architecture designed and is ready to build.

### Days 4–7: Build in parallel

**Person 1:**
- Block internal views: stakeholder map, funding matrix, objection cards, timeline
- Embedding pipeline (auto-embed on insert via Edge Function)
- Semantic search Edge Function endpoint

**Person 2:**
- Continues data population toward targets
- Priority order: objections + audience packets (the chatbot needs these most), then stakeholders, then funding sources, then precedents and timeline

**Person 3:**
- Builds chatbot Edge Function: classification → retrieval → generation pipeline
- Builds chat UI component with audience toggle
- Tests against whatever data is in the database

**End of day 7:** First end-to-end test. Real question → real data → real answer.

### Days 8–10: Integration and testing

**All three together:**
- Run 10–15 test questions across all blocks and audience modes
- Person 2 fills gaps the chatbot exposes ("it couldn't answer X because we're missing Y")
- Person 3 tunes prompts based on output quality
- Person 1 connects chatbot responses to block views (click a citation → see the card)

### Days 11–14: Polish and deliver

**Person 1:** Final frontend pass. Deploy.
**Person 2:** Quality review of all entries. Writes a "how to add data" guide for future contributors.
**Person 3:** Edge case handling. Output formatting (exportable one-page brief). Final prompt quality pass.

## 10. What We're Not Building (Yet)

To stay in scope:
- **No automated monitoring.** Timing/trigger block is manually updated. No news scraping.
- **No strategy simulation.** The chatbot handles cross-cutting questions, but no formal scenario planner.
- **No user accounts.** Single shared tool, not multi-tenant.
- **No mobile.** Desktop web only.
- **No paid services.** Everything runs on free tiers.

---

## 11. What Success Looks Like

At the end of 2 weeks:

1. Land on the platform → see 5 clearly labeled blocks
2. Click into any block → browse structured, sourced data
3. Open the chatbot → select your role (organizer / staffer / official)
4. Ask a question → get a structured answer that pulls from the right blocks, cites real sources, adjusts for audience
5. Click a citation → see the underlying data in the block view

If those 5 things work, the thesis is proven: structured civic knowledge + AI retrieval is more useful than a pile of PDFs.
